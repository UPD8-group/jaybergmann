The one that matters most

Trudy

trudy.org

This one started with the why, not the what.

I have recordings of my mother from seven years ago. I can still hear her voice whenever I want to — and I know exactly what it's worth, because I cared for her through her final months and I'm already forgetting the sound of her in my own memory.

Then today a friend told me she had recordings of her father, who died fifteen years ago. A phone update wiped them. They're simply gone. There is no getting them back. That's the moment this stopped being an idea and became something I had to build — because almost no one has these recordings, and the few who do can lose them to something as ordinary as a software update.

So Trudy captures the life stories of older people — by phone. There's no app, no screen, no login, nothing to learn. The phone rings on the same day each week, an AI interviewer asks gentle questions, and the conversation is recorded, transcribed, and kept forever in a private family vault — safe, backed up, and not sitting on someone's phone waiting to vanish.

The person paying is the adult child — the Curator. The person on the phone is their parent — the Storyteller — who has to do nothing but answer a ringing phone and talk. The voice is preserved exactly as it is. The accent, the pauses, the way they tell a story they've told a hundred times.

It's named after my mother, Trudy — a nurse and midwife. No family should have to lose someone's voice twice.

There's a second product hiding inside the first. The weekly call itself becomes something an older person looks forward to — a reason to remember, a sense that their story matters. That's where Trudy reaches past families into aged care, home care, and social prescribing: purpose as the product, not just preservation.

The things I've built on the side

Small problems, shipped real

I don't build apps to fill a portfolio. Each one started as a real moment of friction — usually one I was standing in. Self-taught, built from my phone, deployed live.

holyshit.app

HolyShit.app

Why I built it: Because finding a clean public toilet when you've got kids in the car and someone's about to lose it is genuinely hard — and every existing tool is a buried government spreadsheet or a Google pin with no information. So I made the best free toilet finder in Australia, gave it a gospel-meets-Michelin-Guide sense of humour, and put it out with no ads, no accounts, no catch.

generalboarding.com

General Boarding

Why I built it: The app that hears your boarding call so you don't have to. I was standing at a gate watching the priority/general boarding signs and thinking about how much quiet anxiety there is in flying — straining to catch a garbled announcement, terrified of missing it. Every existing tool is locked to one airline or one airport. This is the airline-agnostic, anxiety-first layer a traveller keeps across every trip, worldwide. Gate seven, the day I bought it. Seven was Mum's birthday.

saygday.ai

Say G'day

Why I built it: An AI chat assistant for Australian small businesses, trained on their own website, answering customer questions 24/7 with every conversation saved to a simple dashboard. It started because a friend running a Christmas shop couldn't be at her desk at 10pm on a Sunday when someone wanted to know if she was open tomorrow. Now her chatbot answers for her. A front desk that never closes, for the price of a coffee a day.

santasecret.com.au

Santa's Secret

Why I built it: A bespoke Christmas decoration shop in Bungendore, run by Kim. This one's simple — a mate needed a proper web presence and a chatbot named Holly to handle out-of-hours questions, so I made her one. Client work, real and live. Proof the studio tools work for someone other than me.

A little background

The short version

Twenty-plus years in the Australian Public Service — Defence, Finance, COMCAR. I drove Canberra's light rail. I built a Canberra history community on Facebook that reached 80,000 people before a local media company acquired it — so I started another, Lost. Canberra, now just past 2,000 and growing. I like building communities. I like helping people. I've written poetry for forty years, released music, published a novel, and shipped more than a dozen live apps — all self-taught, most of it from a phone.

Two years sober. I cared for my mother through her last eight months. The work I'm proudest of all points the same direction: holding onto the things that quietly vanish. Trudy is where that finally became a business.
