What I want to spend my next decade on

Trudy

This one started with the why, not the what.

In 2018, my mother was diagnosed with a rare and aggressive stage four stomach cancer. She'd just come home from walking the Camino de Santiago — three months, from Saint-Jean, on her own. Getting that news was devastating. But she was a nurse and a midwife. She knew exactly what was coming.

She asked me to be her primary carer. For the next eight months I looked after her — I did everything I possibly could, while watching her die. On the 22nd of February, she passed away while I was holding her hand.

In that time, I recorded conversations with her — stories, memories, moments I knew I wanted to keep, and that I wanted my daughter to have one day. I still have them. I can still hear her voice. And I know exactly what that's worth, because not long ago a friend told me she'd had recordings of her own father, who died fifteen years ago — and a phone update wiped them. They're simply gone. There's no getting them back.

That's the moment this stopped being an idea and became the thing I had to build. Because almost no one has these recordings — and the few who do can lose them to something as ordinary as a software update.

I bought trudy.org a few years ago. I didn't know yet what it would become — only that the name was special, and that one day it would be used for something that mattered. This is it.

So Trudy captures the life stories of older people — by phone. There's no app, no screen, no login, nothing to learn. The phone rings on the same day each week, an AI interviewer asks gentle questions, and the conversation is recorded, transcribed, and kept forever in a private family vault — safe, backed up, and not sitting on someone's phone waiting to vanish.

How it works

The person paying is the adult child — the Curator. The person on the phone is their parent — the Storyteller — who has to do nothing but answer a ringing phone and talk. The voice is preserved exactly as it is. The accent, the pauses, the way they tell a story they've told a hundred times.

The Curator has a simple dashboard. That's where they shape the conversation — choosing the themes and questions the AI will gently work through each week. "Ask Mum about how she met Dad." "Find out what her childhood home was like." "I want to know about the year she spent overseas." So the interview isn't generic; it's guided by the family, toward the stories they most want kept. The Storyteller never sees any of it — for them it's just a warm, curious voice that calls and asks the right questions. The adult child is quietly authoring the record of their own parent's life.

But the recording is only half of it. The call comes at the same time every week, and that rhythm matters. It becomes something to look forward to — a standing appointment, a familiar voice, a reason to reach back into your life and tell it to someone who's listening. From what I understand, routine and a sense of purpose genuinely matter for older people's wellbeing, especially for those who are isolated. So Trudy isn't only preserving a life for the family who comes after — it's giving the person living it a bit of connection and meaning while they're still here to feel it.

A note on how it's built. The thing that makes a service like this work is a voice that feels human enough that an older person forgets they're talking to a machine and just talks. The benchmark there is Sesame's Maya. Their full assistant is proprietary, but the model underneath it — CSM — is open and commercially usable under an Apache 2.0 licence. CSM handles the voice; it doesn't think, so you pair it with a language model for the actual interview. It isn't built for fast, real-time back-and-forth yet — but Trudy doesn't need that. A gentle, unhurried weekly conversation is exactly the kind of pace it's suited to. So the path is real: an open voice layer, a language model guiding the questions, and the call itself wrapped in telephony so the Storyteller needs nothing but a phone that rings.

It's named after my mother, Trudy — a nurse and midwife. No family should have to lose someone's voice twice.

HolyShit.app

Why I built it: Because finding a clean public toilet when you've got kids in the car and someone's about to lose it is genuinely hard — and every existing tool is a buried government spreadsheet or a Google pin with no information. So I made the best free toilet finder in Australia, gave it a gospel-meets-Michelin-Guide sense of humour, and put it out with no ads, no accounts, no catch.

generalboarding.com →

General Boarding

Why I built it: The app that hears your boarding call so you don't have to. I was standing at a gate watching the priority and general boarding signs, thinking about how much quiet anxiety there is in flying — straining to catch a garbled announcement, terrified of missing it. Every existing tool is locked to one airline or one airport. This is the airline-agnostic, anxiety-first layer a traveller keeps across every trip, worldwide. Gate seven, the day I bought it. Seven was Mum's birthday.

saygday.ai →

Say G'day

Why I built it: An AI chat assistant for Australian small businesses, trained on their own website, answering customer questions 24/7 with every conversation saved to a simple dashboard. It started because a friend running a Christmas shop couldn't be at her desk at 10pm on a Sunday when someone wanted to know if she was open tomorrow. Now her chatbot answers for her — a front desk that never closes, for the price of a coffee a day.

If you want the real me

The things I make when no one's asking

I've written poetry for forty years. Recently I turned it into music — atmospheric electronic pieces I release as Vienna Park.

Two years ago I got sober. I tie it to my mother's birthday, the 7th of April — so each year on that day I remember her, and I'm quietly proud of myself for stopping. In that first year, to stay distracted and to finally do something I'd always meant to, I wrote a book: The Reset. I started it on the 7th of April and finished it on the 7th of April a year later. A dozen publishers passed on it. I wasn't going to let that be the end of it, so I published it myself on Amazon.

In the year 2607, reality is programmable. But memory is still painfully human.

It follows Josh Miller — once a renowned terraformer, now shelving rare books beneath a Berlin scarred by climate collapse and draped in augmented illusions. He's haunted by fragments of a past he can't quite remember and a woman he's never met yet somehow loves. When "flickers" of impossible memories start fracturing his world, he's pulled into a question he can't escape: in a place where consciousness itself can be programmed, how do you know if your choices — even your love — are truly your own? Josh's flickers are woven from my own lived experience, which is what gives them their pulse. It's Book One of the TRUDY Trilogy — her name carries on there too.

A little background

The short version

I'm fifty-three, and recently diagnosed with ADHD — which, as far as I'm concerned, is a superpower. It's where all these ideas come from. The difference now is that with AI and vibe-coding, I can finally put them into motion fast — I build alongside Claude most days, turning things that used to stay stuck in my head into live products in a weekend.

I like building communities, and I like helping people — I think it matters to be connected to the place you live. A few years ago I built a Canberra history community on Facebook that reached 80,000 people before a local media company acquired it. I've recently started another, Lost. Canberra, now past 2,000 and growing.

I've written poetry for forty years, released music, published a novel, and shipped more than a dozen live apps — all self-taught, most of it from my phone.

So why Frontier, and why me? The honest answer is that ADHD gives me more ideas than any one person should have, and AI finally lets me build them — but I've been doing it alone, from my phone, in the gaps. The thing I've never had is people around me. A room of others at the same stage, pushing each other every Friday, a network to open doors, the room to chase one idea properly instead of starting six. I build fast on my own; I think I'd build something that lasts with the right people beside me. That's what Frontier means to me.

Thanks for taking the time.
